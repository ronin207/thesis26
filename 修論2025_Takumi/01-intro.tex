\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background}

Post-quantum anonymous credentials answer two pressures at once. The credibility of digital credentials and identity attestations is under strain: a single Indian university reportedly sold roughly $36{,}000$ fake degrees~\cite{scmp2021manavbharti}, forged admission letters have driven student deportations~\cite{bbc2023canada}, and the $2021$ LinkedIn data scrape put approximately $700$ million user profiles up for sale on the dark web~\cite{nixon2021linkedin}, so a credential system must be at once \emph{verifiable} and \emph{privacy-preserving}. This is now a regulatory requirement and not only an academic one: the European Digital Identity framework (eIDAS~2.0, Regulation~(EU)~2024/1183) mandates privacy-preserving attribute attestation with unlinkability for the EU Digital Identity Wallet~\cite{eidas2024}. A credential meeting it must also be \emph{long-lived}, secure against adversaries equipped with large-scale quantum computers, a requirement that rules out classical anonymous-credential constructions whose security reduces to the discrete-logarithm or RSA assumption and that motivates the migration to standardised post-quantum primitives~\cite{fips204,fips205}. Constructions meeting all three requirements at once are only now emerging~\cite{jeudy2023lattice,10.1007/978-981-96-0957-4_3}, and the post-quantum signatures on which such systems are built, with verification cheap enough to prove in zero knowledge, are themselves an active line~\cite{cryptoeprint:2024/868,cryptoeprint:2025/061,cryptoeprint:2024/131}. \emph{Constructing} such a system is the prior question, taken up by those works; this thesis takes up the next one, whether such a system can actually be \emph{deployed}.

Any such credential proves, in zero knowledge, that a post-quantum signature verifies, and that proof must be generated on whatever machine the credential holder owns. The dominant approach compiles the verification predicate into a fixed arithmetic circuit and proves it with a transparent zkSNARK such as Aurora~\cite{cryptoeprint:2018/828}. This specialisation is efficient, but a deployment is not static: the signature scheme is updated as standards mature, security parameters are retuned, and disclosure policies evolve, and a circuit fixed at deployment time must be regenerated and re-audited on each such change. A general-purpose zero-knowledge virtual machine (zkVM)~\cite{cryptoeprint:2023/1032,bruestle2023risczero,yang2026sokzkvm} is an alternative substrate, in which a single universal relation proves the correct execution of an ordinary program, so a change to the predicate becomes a program edit rather than a new circuit. These two substrates trade different costs, and which to choose for deployment on consumer hardware is a live question that the literature has not measured.

The decision belongs to the operator of a real system, a university consortium issuing digital diplomas choosing a proving substrate today, who needs to know whether holders can generate the privacy-protecting proof on their own machines, and what each future scheme or parameter change will cost in regenerated and re-audited artefacts. This thesis answers both by measurement, taking the BDEC anonymous-credential framework~\cite{10.1007/978-981-96-0957-4_3} instantiated with the PLUM signature~\cite{10.1007/978-981-95-2961-2_6} as the concrete workload: BDEC-PLUM is the use case, the comparison of proving substrates is the approach, and the dual obstruction it reveals is the finding.

\subsection{The Current State of Post-Quantum Anonymous Credentials}

Two representative lines of work have attempted to construct post-quantum anonymous-credential systems (the broader lattice-native and PQ-token literature is surveyed in Section~\ref{sec:related}). The first follows the lattice-based approach exemplified by Jeudy et al.\ at Crypto~2023~\cite{jeudy2023lattice}, leveraging the hardness of the Short Integer Solution (SIS) and Learning With Errors (LWE) problems. While this approach achieves quantum resistance, it currently supports only a single message per credential and yields large proof and key sizes that make repeated showings cumbersome.

The second line of work, exemplified by the recent Blockchain-based Digital Education Credential (BDEC) framework of Li, Zhang et al.~\cite{10.1007/978-981-96-0957-4_3}, takes a symmetric-primitive-based approach. BDEC instantiates a generic anonymous-credential construction with the Loquat post-quantum signature scheme~\cite{cryptoeprint:2024/868}, whose verification predicate is comparatively cheap to encode in rank-1 constraint systems (R1CS), and the Aurora transparent zkSNARK~\cite{cryptoeprint:2018/828}, which proves the R1CS relation in zero-knowledge. The resulting system achieves unforgeability, anonymity, unlinkability, conditional linkability, and revocability, and, per the authors' analysis, outperforms the lattice-based approach~\cite{10.1007/978-981-96-0957-4_3}.

A more recent post-quantum signature, PLUM~\cite{10.1007/978-981-95-2961-2_6}, is a successor to Loquat that replaces the Legendre PRF with a $t$-th power residue PRF and the FRI low-degree test with the STIR protocol. At the 128-bit security level, PLUM signatures are approximately $1.5\times$ smaller than Loquat's, and PLUM verification is expected to be up to $4\times$ faster, while requiring roughly $1.28\times$ fewer R1CS constraints~\cite{10.1007/978-981-95-2961-2_6}. Because PLUM shares Loquat's overall structure, a symmetric-primitive PRF assumption verified through a transparent low-degree test, while reporting lower verification cost at matching security, it is the signature primitive we adopt for the zkVM instantiation studied in this thesis. PLUM is a research signature, not yet standardised, chosen for the low R1CS constraint count of its verification predicate. The NIST post-quantum signature standards (ML-DSA, SLH-DSA) and the forthcoming FN-DSA remain the deployment baselines. Their lattice and hash verification predicates are precisely the ones that are expensive to arithmetise, and that is why a symmetric-primitive design is studied here.

\subsection{The Hidden Cost: Recompilation and Re-audit on Each Change}

Both Loquat and PLUM are designed to be efficiently verifiable inside a zkSNARK; in BDEC the verification predicate is compiled into a fixed R1CS for the Aurora transparent argument at deployment time, and this specialisation is what makes the system efficient. Specialisation has a deployment-lifecycle cost that is rarely measured. Any change to the verification \emph{relation}, whether substituting the signature scheme (Loquat~$\to$~PLUM, or a future~$\Sig^\prime$), retuning a security parameter, or changing the presentation \emph{structure}, requires recompilation of the R1CS matrices and re-derivation of any parameters that depend on the constraint count, and a deployment that tracks evolving standards, institutional policies, and threat models incurs this cost repeatedly. (A presentation \emph{policy} that only changes which condition the verifier checks on the disclosed attributes, at fixed structure, is handled verifier-side and forces no recompilation.) We refer to this hidden cost as the \emph{recompilation-and-re-audit cost} of the static-circuit approach, and to its quantitative manifestation as \emph{update churn}. The per-change recompile wall-clock turns out to be small (Section~\ref{ssec:churn-eval}); what the static regime pays per change is a regeneration-and-re-audit footprint, and characterising that footprint, rather than presupposing a wall-clock gap, is the form in which rigidity enters this thesis's evaluation.

\subsection{Zero-Knowledge Virtual Machines as a Structural Alternative}

A zero-knowledge virtual machine (zkVM) is a zkSNARK whose underlying relation is fixed once, at design time of the prover, to the correctness of executing a program on a deterministic instruction-set architecture~\cite{cryptoeprint:2023/1032,bruestle2023risczero,lavin2024surveyapplicationszeroknowledgeproofs}. Anything that a developer can express in a high-level language becomes a guest program; the proof system is universal. Updating the verification predicate becomes a guest-program edit under a fixed universal relation. The audit obligation does not vanish, since the guest binary, its image ID, the host I/O binding, and any precompile interface still require review, but it changes in kind: from auditing a \emph{new relation-specific circuit} to auditing \emph{software and its image under the same, unchanged universal relation}.

In exchange for this generality, a zkVM pays a cost on algebraic primitives whose native arithmetic does not match the zkVM's prover field. PLUM verification involves tens of thousands of explicit multiplications over a large prime field, beside the algebraic hash whose every permutation is itself large-field arithmetic (PLUM's specification labels this field $192$-bit, though the concrete modulus we instantiate is $199$ bits; see Remark~\ref{rem:prime-substitution}), while modern zkVM provers operate over $31$-bit native fields (BabyBear, $2^{31}-2^{27}+1$, in RISC~Zero; KoalaBear, $2^{31}-2^{24}+1$, in the SP1 fork evaluated here); Loquat is analogous, over the quadratic extension $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$ of the $127$-bit Mersenne prime field. Each large-field operation must therefore be emulated by multi-limb arithmetic over the small field. We refer to this cost as the \emph{field-mismatch tax}. Without mitigation, the field-mismatch tax can render PQ-anon-credentials-in-zkVM impractical on consumer hardware: in our measurements the precompile-free standalone PLUM verification exhausts memory (OOM) on the SP1 emulation arm on Apple M-series hardware with $24$~GB, and the full credential relation's succinct proof did not complete, in an anomaly examined in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

Both RISC Zero~\cite{bruestle2023risczero} and SP1~\cite{succinct2024sp1} permit \emph{application-defined precompiles}: native sub-circuits for designated primitives that the prover recognises as syscalls. A precompile can absorb the field-mismatch tax for a primitive that would otherwise dominate the trace. The question, then, is whether a precompile suite covering the algebraic primitives of PLUM verification can make PLUM-instantiated BDEC tractable inside a zkVM on consumer hardware, and how the resulting system compares to the static-circuit baseline along the dimensions that actually matter for deployment.

\subsection{Contributions of This Thesis}

This thesis asks whether a general-purpose zkVM is a viable substrate for post-quantum anonymous credentials on consumer hardware, and answers: \emph{not yet}. On the target hardware the proof one can afford to generate, the standalone PLUM-verification succinct receipt, is not zero-knowledge, while the only zero-knowledge wraps the SP1 toolchain exposes are pairing-based, hence not post-quantum, and the attempted PLONK wrap does not complete on the stack as shipped, blocked by the recursion-shape provisioning wall rather than by a memory bound; on RISC~Zero the succinct receipt carries zero-knowledge blinding but no formally established zero-knowledge guarantee, and its pairing wrap does not run on this hardware at all. The provisioning wall is structural rather than a memory bound: it is surmountable only by a one-time regeneration of the toolchain's static recursion artifacts, not by adding memory; the wrap inventory is a fact about the deployed toolchains that no timing number moves. We call this the \emph{dual obstruction}, and it is the central finding. Around it, two further measurements falsify natural predictions: the bespoke algebraic-hash precompile proves \emph{slower} than a software SHA-3 control at matched security, and the zkVM's flexibility advantage over the transparent static circuit never appears in wall-clock. The contributions below are the artifact, the measurements, and the analysis that locate exactly where the wall sits and what would move it.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A PLUM-instantiated BDEC artifact and its frontier measurements.} We express the BDEC credential-generation (CreGen) relation as a guest program on RISC Zero with PLUM in place of Loquat, realise the full ShowCre presentation relation as a $k+2$-verification guest, and run standalone PLUM signature verification on SP1. In execute mode at $\lambda=80$, CreGen executes both of its signature verifications to acceptance in $9.4\times10^{9}$ guest cycles, and ShowCre measures $1.41\times10^{10}$ cycles at $k=1$ and $1.88\times10^{10}$ at $k=2$, confirming the additive cost model. In prove mode on the target hardware (Apple M5 Pro, $24$~GB), standalone PLUM verification proves in $14.89$ to $32.5$ minutes (build- and machine-state-sensitive; Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}) on SP1 at $\lambda=80$, under the documented reduced-memory configuration, default settings exhausting memory; on RISC~Zero the CreGen succinct prove terminated in an internal-verifier anomaly distinct from a resource limit (under diagnosis, Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}, with no claim of this thesis resting on it), whereas on SP1 both BDEC relations complete a succinct, non-zero-knowledge prove, CreGen in $26.98$ and $31.11$~min and ShowCre in $40.15$ and $44.24$~min at $k{=}1$ and $58.14$~min at $k{=}2$. We report cycle counts, wall-clock times, peak resident-set sizes, and prove and verify times where proving completes, with parameters and procedures documented for reproducibility. To our knowledge this is the first realisation of PLUM-based BDEC components in execute mode and the first standalone PLUM-verify prove-mode measurement inside a general-purpose zkVM. 

  \item \textbf{The deployability wall: a dual obstruction to post-quantum anonymity.} Neither BDEC relation yields a zero-knowledge \emph{proof} on the target hardware, and we locate why. The measured frontier datum is the wrap obstruction: at default concurrency the PLONK wrap of the standalone PLUM-verification receipt is killed at roughly $20$~GB of the $24$~GB budget, but this is a concurrency artifact rather than a memory bound: throttled, the wrap runs well inside the budget and fails instead at recursive compression, where the recursion shapes are provisioned for smaller relations than PLUM verification; the ShowCre wrap, larger, is a fortiori unreached. The receipt one can generate is succinct but not zero-knowledge on SP1, whose only zero-knowledge wraps are pairing-based and so not post-quantum; on RISC~Zero the succinct receipt is blinded but carries no formally established zero-knowledge guarantee, and the pairing wrap is unavailable on Apple silicon. Deployable post-quantum anonymity is therefore not reached on this stack, by a route that is the recursion-shape provisioning wall rather than a memory limit, a structural fact that more memory does not remove.

  \item \textbf{The precompile as instrument, a falsified speedup, the wall it builds, and the cost model behind them.} We design and implement a Griffin-$\mathbb{F}_p^{192}$ precompile~\cite{cryptoeprint:2022/403} (a bespoke AIR on SP1; on RISC~Zero the same arithmetic is carried by \texttt{sys\_bigint}) for the algebraic-hash primitive that the cost analysis of Section~\ref{sec:theoretical} identifies as the dominant tax-bearing operation, integrating it with the zkVMs' existing big-integer modular-multiplication precompiles (SP1's \texttt{UINT256\_MUL} and RISC~Zero's \texttt{sys\_bigint})~\cite{succinct2024sp1,bruestle2023risczero}. Against the precompile-free path, which exhausts memory at $\approx$1m45s (Table~\ref{tab:plum-verify}), the precompile turns standalone PLUM verification into the finite $14.89$-to-$32.5$-minute measurement above; introducing a custom AIR adds a trusted component to the verification path, so the security statement is \emph{conditional} on the constraint-level soundness of the Griffin AIR, carried as open Assumption~\ref{asm:air} in Section~\ref{sec:security}. The precompile does not, however, deliver a speedup where one was expected: at matched $\lambda=80$ the Griffin arm proves slower than a PLUM arm using a software SHA-3 hasher and the big-integer precompile alone in every run we took, on both the May and the June builds. The sign of this inversion is robust; its magnitude is build-sensitive, from $1.10\times$ in a same-session June pair to $2.45\times$ across the May builds, on the metric on which deployment cost depends. We report this inversion as a substantive finding, and the trace-area cost model of Section~\ref{sec:theoretical} postdicts its sign together with the feasibility recovery: a precompile pays exactly when the core trace area it removes exceeds the chip area it adds, an inequality these two measurements evaluate at opposite operating points. A third measurement makes the theme structural: the Griffin chip that recovers feasibility is the sole cause of the recursion-shape provisioning wall that blocks the zero-knowledge wrap (Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}). Removing only the chip restores the pre-precompile recursion shape byte-for-byte; with it, the shape grows by $34.8\%$ and overflows the static provisioning the wrap depends on. The instrument that recovers feasibility at the core layer is the one that bills the recursion layer, and the field-mismatch tax it removes from the guest reappears, transformed, one layer up.

  \item \textbf{A secondary dimension: \emph{update churn} and a deployment decision framework.} We define update churn quantitatively as the regeneration footprint a localised change to the verification relation forces: the set of compiled artefacts and proof-system parameters that must be regenerated and re-audited. We enumerate this footprint for both regimes and measure the recompile cost it was presumed to impose. Against the transparent, non-preprocessing Aurora baseline the presumed wall-clock advantage of the zkVM is absent (the static recompile is sub-second), surviving only as a regeneration-and-re-audit footprint; against a preprocessing baseline (Fractal) the advantage is real and large, the per-circuit indexing itself exceeding the memory budget at the BDEC size. From these measurements and the frontier above we give a decision framework mapping deployment-scenario parameters (policy-update frequency, hardware budget, anonymity-strength requirement) onto a regime recommendation, separating the dimensions on which the comparison is conclusive from those bounded by the consumer-hardware proving frontier.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope and Non-Goals}

This thesis is concerned with the practical deployability of PQ anonymous credentials on consumer hardware. We therefore make the following scope choices.

We focus on the BDEC framework~\cite{10.1007/978-981-96-0957-4_3} as the anonymous-credential construction, because (i) it is the leading symmetric-primitive-based PQ anonymous-credential system in the recent literature, (ii) its structure of two signature-verification predicates under a hidden user public key inside a zkSNARK is representative of a broad class of PQ-anon-cred designs, and (iii) its reliance on Aurora makes the static-circuit baseline well-defined and reproducible.

We focus on RISC Zero and SP1 as the zkVM substrates, because both expose application-defined precompiles, both target consumer hardware, and both serve as representative examples of the current generation of RISC-V-based zkVMs.

We do \emph{not} propose a new post-quantum signature scheme: we take PLUM as given and our contribution lies in how it is embedded into the broader credential system. We do \emph{not} propose new cryptographic assumptions: the security of the resulting system reduces to those already established for Loquat, PLUM, BDEC, RISC Zero, and SP1, together with a small number of premises that we state explicitly and carry as \emph{open} where they arise (the constraint-level soundness of the Griffin AIR, the existence of a PLUM signature-transcript simulator, and the quantum-random-oracle lifts), discharged in Section~\ref{sec:security} only as far as those references allow. We do \emph{not} claim that consumer-hardware PQ anonymous credentials are universally deployable today; rather, we quantify the cost frontier and identify the load-bearing pieces of the cost.

\subsection{Thesis Organisation}

The remainder of this thesis is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related work on post-quantum signatures, anonymous credentials, zkSNARKs, zkVMs, and precompile design. Section~\ref{sec:prelim} establishes the cryptographic primitives, signature schemes, zkSNARK and zkVM definitions, the BDEC construction, and the informal notion of update churn used throughout the thesis. Section~\ref{sec:theoretical} gives the formal definition of update churn and formalises the question we ask of each system regime and the comparative analysis we apply. Section~\ref{sec:system} describes the design and implementation of PLUM-in-BDEC in both the static-circuit and the zkVM regimes, including the precompile suite. Section~\ref{sec:security} states the conditional security-preservation theorem, its two open assumptions (Griffin-AIR soundness and PLUM transcript simulatability), and the folded lookup-binding and black-box-transfer hypotheses. Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} reports the empirical measurements on consumer hardware. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the measurements as a deployment-lifecycle decision framework. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes and identifies open problems.
