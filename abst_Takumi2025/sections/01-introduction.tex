\section{Introduction}
Post-quantum anonymous credentials let a holder prove possession of a valid
credential without revealing identity, under assumptions that survive a
quantum adversary. We target the setting in which the holder generates this
proof on a \emph{personal device}, here a consumer laptop (18-core, 24\,GB),
as opposed to a cloud or server prover. A general-purpose zero-knowledge
virtual machine (zkVM) proves an arbitrary verification predicate without
a bespoke, per-scheme circuit, at the cost of a generality tax on the arithmetic. The obstacle is arithmetic. The schemes we
care about (PLUM~\cite{10.1007/978-981-95-2961-2_6}, Loquat~\cite{cryptoeprint:2024/868}) operate over a nominally $192$-bit (concretely $199$-bit) prime field, while
production zkVMs (RISC~Zero~\cite{bruestle2023risczero}, SP1~\cite{succinct2024sp1}) fix a small ($\approx 31$-bit) native
field, so every operation is emulated by multi-limb arithmetic; an algebraic
hash (Griffin~\cite{cryptoeprint:2022/403}), which makes up $\approx 91\%$ of the verification's R1CS
constraints at PLUM-128~\cite[\S4.2]{10.1007/978-981-95-2961-2_6}, amplifies the penalty. This thesis asks \emph{when} a custom precompile pays for compiling such a
signature into a zkVM; using that criterion it reduces the proving cost of the
verification on a personal device, and it locates where a jointly zero-knowledge
and post-quantum proof is still out of reach.
