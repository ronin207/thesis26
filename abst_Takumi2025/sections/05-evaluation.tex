\section{Evaluation}
We measure four cells on the target hardware: PLUM verification in a zkVM
(1)~without and (2)~with the Griffin precompile; (3)~the same verification
using software SHA-3 as a control; and (4)~Loquat verification in an
application-specific circuit (Aurora) as an in-SNARK reference (the
measured static baseline; PLUM over its $199$-bit field has no Aurora
harness).

Without the precompile, verification does not terminate within a
$24\,\mathrm{GB}/3\,\mathrm{h}$ bound ($\approx 7.08$ billion cycles at
$\lambda=80$, projecting to tens of hours). With the precompile it completes
in $14.25$~min (mean of three fixed-config runs, $14.05$--$14.61$) on the
$24\,\mathrm{GB}$ machine at a peak ${\approx}14.2\,\mathrm{GB}$ resident, the first
configuration in the study to fit the budget.

The control is the sharpest result. Even with the precompile, PLUM
verification executes $1.13\times$ more cycles ($125.5$\,M vs $110.8$\,M) and
proves about $1.09\times$ slower in wall-clock ($14.25$ vs $13.05$~min, mean of
three): a standardized hash, unaccelerated, still beats our accelerated
algebraic hash. This is the
\emph{precompile paradox} made empirical: the cost of choosing a
bespoke algebraic-hash primitive over one the ecosystem has standardized.
